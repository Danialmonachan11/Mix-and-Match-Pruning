\begin{abstract}
Deploying deep neural networks (DNNs) on edge platforms requires aggressive compression of the model architecture with minimal accuracy loss. This paper introduces Mix-and-Match Pruning. The name reflects the
core principle: \textit{Mixing} multiple baseline pruning criteria
to establish layer-wise sparsity ranges, then \textit{Matching}
these ranges to the architecture by systematically sampling diverse sparsity configuration. It is a globally guided, layer-wise sparsification framework that systematically coordinates multiple pruning criteria to produce architecture-adaptive sparsity profiles. Rather than proposing yet another pruning rule, the framework addresses a fundamental but overlooked challenge: different layers, architectures, and application domains respond differently to each pruning criterion, and no single method is universally optimal. By exploring layer sensitivity ranges and combining complementary global criteria, Mix-and-Match Pruning generates a family of high-quality sparse models spanning diverse accuracy–sparsity trade-offs. Experiments on CNNs and Vision Transformers show substantial improvements in robustness at extreme sparsity. On Swin-Tiny, the method reduces accuracy degradation by 40\% relative to standard single-criterion pruning while achieving 42\% model sparsity. The results demonstrate that rethinking how pruning criteria are applied—rather than inventing new ones—unlocks significantly more reliable and deployment-ready compressed models.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Deep neural networks have achieved strong performance across diverse vision tasks including image classification, object detection, and semantic segmentation, but face critical deployment challenges on edge devices with strict memory ($<$10 MB), computational (GFLOPs), and power constraints \cite{chavan2024edgeai, promwad2024compression}. Modern CNNs such as ResNet-50 typically require 100--200 MB, while larger vision transformers can exceed 300--500 MB, rendering direct deployment on edge devices infeasible. While compression techniques including quantization \cite{jacob2018quantization}, knowledge distillation \cite{hinton2015distilling}, and neural architecture search \cite{zoph2017neural} exist, neural network pruning has proven particularly effective by exploiting redundancy in overparameterized networks to achieve substantial parameter reductions while maintaining acceptable accuracy \cite{liu2024memory, zhang2024recap}.

Pruning methods have evolved from magnitude-based pruning (MAG) \cite{han2015learning} that removes smallest-magnitude weights, to Single-shot Network Pruning (SNIP) \cite{lee2018snip} using gradient-weight products, Gradient Signal Preservation (GraSP) \cite{wang2020picking} incorporating Hessian information to preserve gradient flow, semi-structured pruning for vision transformers (LPViT) \cite{wu2025lpvit}, and joint pruning-quantization optimization (GETA) \cite{geta2025}. Each of these represents a different pruning criterion, that is, a rule for deciding which weights to remove, such as weight magnitude, gradient magnitude, gradient flow preservation, or quantization-aware importance scoring. These methods achieve competitive results compared to iterative pruning approaches with minimal computational overhead.

Existing pruning methods produce single-point solutions for specific target sparsities, requiring complete re-execution for different deployment scenarios with varying accuracy-compression requirements. Furthermore, different architectural components respond differently to pruning: skip connections in residual networks require conservative pruning to preserve gradient flow \cite{li2017pruning}, while attention mechanisms in transformers create global dependencies across all tokens, making them more sensitive to aggressive pruning \cite{rao2021dynamicvit}.

Current single-point methods cannot systematically explore this design space. Moreover, empirical evidence demonstrating the effectiveness of multi-strategy pruning frameworks on vision transformers remains limited. Recent works have begun to explore generating multiple pruned configurations \cite{lee2024mpruner, desai2024llamaflex}, yet a unified framework for hybrid architectures that systematically explores the accuracy-compression tradeoff space is still needed.

We propose Mix-and-Match Pruning, a framework that systematically explores families of solutions through a three-phase process. First, it observes baseline pruning behavior to establish architecture-aware sparsity ranges (minimum and maximum pruning levels per layer). Then, it generates diverse sparsity configurations spanning the accuracy-compression tradeoff space. This provides deployment flexibility through multiple operating points without re-executing pruning procedures, achieving substantial compression suitable for edge deployment \cite{wu2025lpvit}.

The main contributions of this paper are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A layer-wise pruning framework that derives architecture-aware sparsity ranges from multiple baseline criteria and systematically generates diverse sparsity configurations through multi-criterion exploration.
    \item A family of pruned models spanning different accuracy--sparsity trade-offs, enabling practitioners to select deployment configurations without re-executing the pruning procedure.
    \item A comprehensive evaluation across CNNs and ViTs, identifying cases in which the proposed approach yields substantial benefits over single-criterion baselines.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section~\ref{sec:method} presents the methodology, Section~\ref{sec:results} provides experimental results and analysis, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

\section{Mix-and-Match Pruning Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
Mix-and-Match Pruning is architecture-agnostic and consists of three phases: range establishment, strategy generation, and pruning execution. In this work, a ``strategy'' refers to a specific layer-wise sparsity configuration (i.e., a target sparsity level for each layer), not a pruning criterion. A pretrained network and a small labeled calibration set (typically 5--10\% of the training data) are provided as inputs, and a family of pruned models is produced as output. The key idea is that established pruning criteria exhibit stable and consistent patterns in layer-wise sparsity distributions, that is, each criterion consistently identifies which layers can tolerate higher pruning and which require preservation, with this behavior remaining consistent across different target sparsity levels and layer types (e.g., convolutional layers, attention blocks, and feed-forward layers in transformers). These patterns are used to construct sparsity ranges that capture natural compression behavior and serve as boundaries for systematic exploration. \subsection{Phase 1: Architecture-Aware Range Establishment}

In the first phase (Algorithm~\ref{alg:multistrategy}, lines 3--12), the method derives layer-wise sparsity ranges for each layer $l$ by applying the user-specified set of pruning criteria at several global sparsity targets. Here, $\rho$ denotes the fraction of weights pruned (set to zero) in a given layer, and the ranges are expressed as $[\rho_{min}^{(l)}, \rho_{max}^{(l)}]$. Let $\mathcal{B}$ denote the set of baseline pruning methods and $\mathcal{T}$ the set of target sparsities. For each method--target pair, the resulting layer-wise sparsity $\rho_{b,t}^{(l)}$ is recorded by running baseline method $b$ at global target $t$ on a small calibration set of labeled data (typically 5--10\% of the training set). Minimum and maximum sparsities across all executions are then computed based purely on observed sparsity distributions, without filtering by accuracy:

\begin{equation}
\rho_{min}^{(l)} = \min \{\rho_{b,t}^{(l)} \mid b \in \mathcal{B}, t \in \mathcal{T}\},
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\rho_{max}^{(l)} = \max \{\rho_{b,t}^{(l)} \mid b \in \mathcal{B}, t \in \mathcal{T}\}.
\end{equation}

These bounds define the empirical range of layer-wise sparsity values observed across different baseline methods, establishing feasible limits for exploration. Accuracy is evaluated later during strategy selection (Phase 3), not during range establishment. Structural constraints (e.g., unprunable normalization layers~\cite{li2017pruning}, minimally pruned small layers, and conservative treatment of transformer patch embeddings~\cite{dosovitskiy2021vit}) are then applied to these ranges, with specific threshold values and their empirical justification presented in Section~\ref{sec:results}.

% These bounds provide conservative, empirically grounded limits for exploration. Structural constraints are applied to preserve functional integrity: normalization layers are assigned fixed ranges of $[0\%, 0\%]$; small layers with fewer than 10K parameters receive $[0\%, 10\%]$; and transformer patch embeddings receive $[15\%, 30\%]$ to maintain essential token representations.


\subsection{Phase 2: Multi-Strategy Generation}

Having established layer-wise sparsity ranges by observing multiple baseline criteria in Phase~1, this phase generates a set of pruning strategies by systematically sampling those ranges (Algorithm~\ref{alg:multistrategy}, lines 13--18). Importantly, each strategy is \emph{not} a mixture of different baseline pruning criteria (e.g., partly MAG and partly SNIP). Instead, each strategy specifies a complete layer-wise sparsity configuration—a target sparsity level for each layer—that defines \emph{where} and \emph{how much} to prune. A pruning criterion applied in Phase~3 then determines \emph{which weights} to prune to achieve these targets. We generate ten strategies, a number chosen to balance diversity and computational cost, each specifying a complete sparsity configuration

\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\rho} = [\rho^{(1)}, \rho^{(2)}, \ldots, \rho^{(L)}],
\end{equation}

where $L$ denotes the total number of prunable layers in the network.

\textbf{Strategies 1--3 (Core bounds):} Three core strategies directly reflect the established bounds: (1) a \emph{conservative} strategy assigns $\rho_{min}^{(l)}$ to every layer, (2) an \emph{aggressive} strategy assigns $\rho_{max}^{(l)}$, and (3) a \emph{balanced} strategy uses the midpoint of the range. These provide lower, upper, and central reference configurations.

\textbf{Strategies 4--7 (Percentile-based):} To explore intermediate behaviours, four percentile-based strategies are generated by interpolating within each layer's range using interpolation coefficient $\alpha \in \{0.3,\,0.5,\,0.7,\,0.9\}$:

\begin{equation}
\rho^{(l)} = \rho_{min}^{(l)} + \alpha\big(\rho_{max}^{(l)} - \rho_{min}^{(l)}\big).
\end{equation}

These values correspond to the 30th, 50th, 70th, and 90th percentiles of the sparsity range, yielding progressively more aggressive sparsity assignments while remaining within empirically validated limits.

\textbf{Strategy 8 (Parameter-proportional):} A parameter-proportional strategy is constructed by scaling each layer's sparsity based on its parameter count:

\begin{equation}
\rho^{(l)} = \rho_{min}^{(l)} + \beta\big(\rho_{max}^{(l)} - \rho_{min}^{(l)}\big),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\beta = \min\left(1.0,\, \frac{|\mathcal{W}^{(l)}|}{|\mathcal{W}_{avg}|} \cdot 0.1\right),
\end{equation}

where $|\mathcal{W}^{(l)}|$ is the number of weights in layer $l$ and $|\mathcal{W}_{avg}|$ is the average number of weights per layer across the network. This biases pruning toward larger layers while preventing over-pruning of small ones.

\textbf{Strategies 9--10 (Structure-aware):} Finally, two strategies aware of network architectural structure,that is, the positional and functional roles of layers, are produced: (9) a \emph{classifier-heavy} variant that allocates higher sparsity to late-stage layers (classification heads), and (10) a \emph{feature-heavy} variant that increases sparsity in early convolutional or embedding layers (feature extractors). Both are generated by adjusting the interpolation coefficient $\alpha$ according to layer depth and type.

Together, these ten strategies provide a diverse, systematically constructed set of pruning configurations spanning conservative, aggressive, intermediate, parameter-aware, and structure-aware pruning behaviours.

\subsection{Phase 3: Pruning Execution}

In the final phase (Algorithm~\ref{alg:multistrategy}, lines 19--29), each strategy is instantiated using sensitivity-based pruning followed by mask application and fine-tuning. Multiple sensitivity formulations may be selected by the user, and each strategy is executed once per chosen formulation, yielding a set of pruned models for comparative evaluation.

\textbf{Sensitivity-Based Pruning.}
Three scoring rules are supported to characterize weight importance, where $i$ indexes individual weights within layer $l$:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textit{Magnitude-only:} \quad $S_{i,\text{mag}}^{(l)} = |w_i^{(l)}|$ \cite{han2015learning}
    \item \textit{Gradient-only:} \quad $S_{i,\text{grad}}^{(l)} = \left|\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w_i^{(l)}}\right|$ \cite{lee2018snip}
    \item \textit{Magnitude--gradient product:} \quad $S_{i,\text{prod}}^{(l)} = |w_i^{(l)}| \cdot \left|\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w_i^{(l)}}\right|$ \cite{wang2020picking}
\end{enumerate}

These formulations quantify weight saliency from complementary perspectives: static structural relevance (magnitude)~\cite{han2015learning}, dynamic training sensitivity (gradient)~\cite{lee2018snip}, and a combined measure reflecting both~\cite{wang2020picking}. Gradients are accumulated over a held-out calibration set and normalized to ensure comparability across layers. After computing the selected sensitivity metric $S_i^{(l)}$ for all weights, weights within each layer are ranked, and the lowest-ranked $\rho^{(l)}$ proportion is pruned using binary masks:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{M}^{(l)} \in \{0,1\}^{|\mathcal{W}^{(l)}|},
\end{equation}

where $\mathcal{M}^{(l)}$ is a binary vector of length $|\mathcal{W}^{(l)}|$ (the number of weights in layer $l$), with each element $\mathcal{M}_i^{(l)}$ set to 1 to retain weight $w_i^{(l)}$ or 0 to prune it.

Executing all selected scoring rules for each strategy produces multiple pruned variants, enabling empirical identification of the most suitable criterion for a given architecture.

\textbf{Mask Application and Fine-Tuning.}
Pruning is applied by zeroing masked weights:

\begin{equation}
\tilde{w}_i^{(l)} = \mathcal{M}_i^{(l)} \cdot w_i^{(l)}.
\end{equation}

Fine-tuning is performed to recover accuracy lost due to pruning. During fine-tuning, masked weights remain fixed at zero by re-applying the binary mask $\mathcal{M}^{(l)}$ after every gradient update step: $w_i^{(l)} \leftarrow \mathcal{M}_i^{(l)} \cdot w_i^{(l)}$, where $i$ indexes individual weights within layer $l$. This ensures pruned weights stay at zero and gradient updates are restricted to unpruned parameters only.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Mix-and-Match Pruning Framework}
\label{alg:multistrategy}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} Pretrained network $\mathcal{N}$, pruning baselines $\mathcal{B}$ (e.g., MAG, SNIP), sparsity targets $\mathcal{T}$, pruning criteria $\mathcal{C}$ (e.g., magnitude, gradient)
\STATE \textbf{Output:} Pruned models $\{\mathcal{N}_{s,c}\}$ where $s$ indexes strategies and $c$ indexes criteria

\STATE \textbf{// Phase 1: Range Establishment}
\FOR{$b \in \mathcal{B}$}
    \FOR{$t \in \mathcal{T}$}
        \STATE Run $b$ at target $t$; record $\rho_{b,t}^{(l)}$
    \ENDFOR
\ENDFOR
\FOR{each layer $l$}
    \STATE $\rho_{min}^{(l)} \gets \min_{b,t}\rho_{b,t}^{(l)}$, \;
           $\rho_{max}^{(l)} \gets \max_{b,t}\rho_{b,t}^{(l)}$
    \STATE Update bounds $[\rho_{min}^{(l)},\rho_{max}^{(l)}]$ with structural constraints (e.g., force $\rho^{(l)}=0$ for BatchNorm)
\ENDFOR

\STATE \textbf{// Phase 2: Strategy Generation}
\STATE $\mathcal{S} \gets \emptyset$
\FOR{each strategy $s$ among the 10 defined types}
    \STATE Form $\boldsymbol{\rho}_s$ by sampling $[\rho_{\min}^{(l)},\rho_{\max}^{(l)}]$
    \STATE $\mathcal{S} \gets \mathcal{S} \cup \{\boldsymbol{\rho}_s\}$
\ENDFOR

\STATE \textbf{// Phase 3: Pruning Execution}
\FOR{each $\boldsymbol{\rho}_s \in \mathcal{S}$}
    \FOR{each criterion $c \in \mathcal{C}$}
        \STATE Compute sensitivity scores $S_i^{(l)}$ under $c$ (with gradient accumulation if needed)
        \FOR{each layer $l$}
            \STATE Rank weights; form mask $\mathcal{M}_{s,c}^{(l)}$ pruning $\rho_s^{(l)}$
        \ENDFOR
        \STATE Apply masks: $\mathcal{N}_{s,c} \gets \mathcal{N} \odot \mathcal{M}_{s,c}$
        \STATE Fine-tune $\mathcal{N}_{s,c}$ with mask enforcement
    \ENDFOR
\ENDFOR

\STATE \textbf{return} $\{\mathcal{N}_{s,c}\}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}



% Algorithm~\ref{alg:multistrategy} presents the complete framework workflow. Lines 5-12 (Phase 1) establish architecture-aware ranges by executing baseline methods at multiple global sparsity targets and computing per-layer minimum and maximum observed sparsities. Lines 15-22 (Phase 2) generate the strategy family through structured sampling of established ranges, producing ten diverse pruning configurations. Lines 24-33 (Phase 3) execute each strategy with sensitivity scores computed once and cached (line 25) to avoid redundant calculations across all ten strategies, followed by mask application, fine-tuning with gradient updates restricted to unpruned weights, and post-training quantization. The framework outputs a family of pruned models spanning the accuracy-compression tradeoff space, enabling deployment flexibility without re-execution.

% \textbf{Computational Considerations.} The range establishment phase (lines 5-12) requires running three baseline methods at multiple sparsity targets, representing a one-time setup cost of approximately three times a single baseline evaluation. However, this cost is amortized across the ten generated strategies and can be reused for future pruning tasks on the same architecture. Importantly, the reported inference FLOPs measure per-forward-pass computational requirements of the final pruned models and are identical across methods because unstructured pruning maintains layer dimensions. Training and setup costs are one-time expenses not included in deployment metrics, following standard practice in pruning literature.

The evaluation covers four representative architectures selected to span classical convolutional, residual, hybrid, and hierarchical transformer designs. These models—VGG-11 \cite{simonyan2015vgg}, ResNet-18 \cite{he2016resnet}, LeViT-384 \cite{graham2021levit}, and Swin-Tiny \cite{liu2021swin}—are paired with datasets commonly used for vision benchmarks. VGG-11 and LeViT-384 are trained on CIFAR-10 \cite{krizhevsky2009cifar}, ResNet-18 on GTSRB \cite{stallkamp2012gtsrb}, and Swin-Tiny on CIFAR-100 \cite{krizhevsky2009cifar}. These baselines provide the reference point against which the effects of the Mix-and-Match Pruning are assessed.

Since unstructured pruning maintains original tensor dimensions, theoretical Floating Point Operations (FLOPs) remain unchanged. Nonetheless, sparsification substantially reduces the memory footprint of the resulting models and enables acceleration on sparse-aware hardware. These properties make the baselines in Table~\ref{tab:baselines} suitable for examining how globally guided, layer-adaptive sparsity interacts with architectural diversity and dataset scale.


All pruned models are fine-tuned to recover accuracy under a unified optimization protocol to ensure comparability across architectures and pruning strategies. The Adam optimizer~\cite{kingma2014adam} is used with a fixed learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-4}$ for VGG-11 and $5 \times 10^{-5}$ for ResNet-18, LeViT-384, and Swin-Tiny (AdamW~\cite{loshchilov2019decoupled} for LeViT-384 with weight decay of 0.05), with a batch size of 128. During fine-tuning, parameter masks are enforced after every gradient update to preserve the imposed sparsity pattern. Early stopping with a patience of 3 epochs is employed to prevent overfitting. The number of fine-tuning epochs is capped at 20 for VGG-11, ResNet-18, and LeViT-384, while Swin-Tiny uses up to 30 epochs due to its hierarchical window-based attention, which demonstrates slower convergence when subjected to high sparsity. Five representative baseline pruning methods are included: MAG (magnitude pruning)~\cite{han2015learning}, which removes weights with the smallest absolute values; SNIP (Single-shot Network Pruning)~\cite{lee2018snip}, which uses connection sensitivity based on the product of gradients and weights before training; GraSP (Gradient Signal Preservation)~\cite{wang2020picking}, which leverages gradient flow and Hessian information to identify and preserve critical connections; LPViT~\cite{wu2025lpvit}, which applies semi-structured pruning optimized for vision transformer architectures; and GETA~\cite{geta2025}, which employs joint optimization with quantization-aware importance scoring. These baselines can be selected by the user within the framework and follow the same fine-tuning configuration to maintain fairness in comparison.

Architecture-aware structural constraints are incorporated into Mix-and-Match Pruning to prevent degradation from overly aggressive sparsification in sensitive components. The specific thresholds used in all experiments are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Normalization layers} (BatchNorm, LayerNorm): fixed at $0\%$ sparsity (unprunable) to preserve statistical stability~\cite{li2017pruning}.
    \item \textbf{Small layers} with fewer than 10K parameters: capped at a conservative range of $[0\%, 10\%]$, as empirical analysis shows that aggressive pruning disproportionately harms accuracy for such layers~\cite{han2015learning}.
    \item \textbf{Transformer patch embeddings}: restricted to $[15\%, 30\%]$ sparsity to maintain token diversity and avoid collapse of early-stage representations~\cite{dosovitskiy2021vit}.
\end{itemize}

These empirically derived ranges guide feasible sparsity exploration and are further analyzed and justified in the results.

The evaluation relies on several metrics that capture sparsification level and predictive performance. Sparsity is defined as the global percentage of weights set to zero across all prunable layers, computed as the ratio of zero-valued weights to the total number of weights. Bias parameters are excluded, and normalization layers (BatchNorm, LayerNorm) are preserved through architecture-aware constraints. Predictive performance is measured using FP32 accuracy after fine-tuning. The accuracy drop is expressed as the difference between the unpruned FP32 baseline and the pruned model.

\subsection{Computational Complexity Analysis}
\label{sec:complexity}

The Mix-and-Match framework introduces a one-time setup cost in Phase~1 (range establishment) that is amortized across the generation of multiple pruning strategies. This section analyzes the computational overhead compared to baseline single-criterion methods.

\textbf{Complexity of Phase 1 (Range Establishment).}
Phase~1 runs each baseline method $b \in \mathcal{B}$ at each target sparsity $t \in \mathcal{T}$ to establish layer-wise sparsity ranges. For a network with $L$ prunable layers, the time complexity is:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{O}_{\text{Phase 1}} = |\mathcal{B}| \times |\mathcal{T}| \times \mathcal{C}_{\text{prune}}(L),
\end{equation}

where $\mathcal{C}_{\text{prune}}(L)$ denotes the cost of executing a single pruning criterion (scoring, ranking, and masking weights across $L$ layers). In our experiments, $|\mathcal{B}| = 3$ (MAG, SNIP, GraSP) and $|\mathcal{T}| = 3$ (70\%, 80\%, 90\% sparsity targets), yielding a multiplicative factor of 9 compared to running a single baseline once.

\textbf{Complexity of Phase 2 (Strategy Generation).}
Phase~2 is negligible, consisting only of interpolation operations within pre-established ranges. Generating all 10 strategies requires $\mathcal{O}(L)$ arithmetic operations and is completed in milliseconds.

\textbf{Complexity of Phase 3 (Pruning Execution).}
Each of the 10 strategies is executed once per selected sensitivity criterion. If $|\mathcal{C}|$ criteria are chosen (e.g., magnitude-only, gradient-only, magnitude-gradient product), the cost is:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{O}_{\text{Phase 3}} = 10 \times |\mathcal{C}| \times \mathcal{C}_{\text{prune+ft}}(L),
\end{equation}

where $\mathcal{C}_{\text{prune+ft}}(L)$ includes pruning, masking, and fine-tuning. Since fine-tuning dominates this cost and is identical across all methods, the per-model overhead is equivalent to that of a single baseline.

\textbf{Comparison with Baselines.}
Running a single baseline method (e.g., MAG) at one target sparsity requires $\mathcal{C}_{\text{prune+ft}}(L)$ operations. To obtain the same diversity of solutions using traditional single-criterion methods, one would need to: (1) run MAG, SNIP, and GraSP separately at various sparsity targets, (2) manually tune per-layer sparsity configurations, and (3) re-execute pruning for each new configuration. The total cost for generating 10 comparable configurations with baselines would be at least $10 \times \mathcal{C}_{\text{prune+ft}}(L)$, plus additional hyperparameter search overhead.

\textbf{Amortized Advantage.}
Mix-and-Match incurs a one-time setup cost of $9 \times \mathcal{C}_{\text{prune}}(L)$ in Phase~1, but produces 10 diverse strategies in Phase~3 at a cost of $10 \times \mathcal{C}_{\text{prune+ft}}(L)$. Since $\mathcal{C}_{\text{prune}} \ll \mathcal{C}_{\text{prune+ft}}$ (scoring and masking are orders of magnitude faster than fine-tuning), the effective per-strategy cost is comparable to running a single baseline. Crucially, the framework provides multi-point exploration (10 strategies spanning the accuracy-sparsity trade-off space without re-execution), architecture-aware bounds (empirically validated sparsity ranges prevent over- or under-pruning), and systematic coverage (strategies are generated deterministically, ensuring reproducibility). In practice, the upfront cost of Phase~1 is justified when practitioners require multiple deployment configurations or wish to explore the Pareto front of accuracy-sparsity trade-offs. For scenarios requiring only a single pruned model, traditional baseline methods remain computationally preferable.

\textbf{Practical Runtime.}
On an NVIDIA A100 GPU, Phase~1 (range establishment) for VGG-11 completes in approximately 15 minutes, while each Phase~3 strategy execution (pruning + fine-tuning for 20 epochs) requires 8--10 minutes. Generating all 10 strategies takes roughly 90 minutes total, comparable to running 9--11 baseline executions individually, but with the added benefit of systematic exploration and architecture-aware sparsity allocation.

The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed Mix-and-Match Pruning framework consistently outperforms baseline methods across all evaluated architectures, including VGG-11, ResNet-18, LeViT-384, and Swin-Tiny. By combining layer-wise sensitivity exploration with adaptive sparsity allocation, the framework identifies pruning configurations that maintain high accuracy even at aggressive sparsity levels.

Layer-wise pruning allows the framework to assign different sparsity levels to each layer based on its sensitivity, preserving crucial features in early convolutional layers, attention heads, or residual connections while aggressively pruning less critical regions. This approach results in higher overall accuracy compared to uniform or single-criterion pruning.  

The results indicate that the optimal sensitivity metric depends on the architecture: a combination of magnitude and gradient is most effective for VGG-11, magnitude alone for ResNet-18 and Swin-Tiny, and gradient alone for LeViT-384. This variation reflects architectural properties: VGG-11's sequential structure benefits from combining learned feature importance (magnitude) with training dynamics (gradient); ResNet-18 and Swin-Tiny's residual connections and hierarchical attention ensure stable gradient flow, making magnitude a reliable indicator of parameter importance; while LeViT-384's hybrid architecture with dynamic attention routing requires gradient-based sensitivity to capture context-dependent parameter utility during training.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
This paper introduced \textit{Mix-and-Match Pruning}, an architecture-aware framework that improves the effectiveness of existing pruning algorithms through layer-wise sensitivity exploration and adaptive sparsity allocation. By systematically combining user-selectable criteria, the method produces consistently lower accuracy drop across CNNs, hybrid models, and Vision Transformers, outperforming representative baselines without requiring new pruning rules or expensive retraining. Empirical results demonstrate the framework's effectiveness: VGG-11 achieves 90.41\% accuracy at 89.60\% sparsity (1.99\% drop), ResNet-18 reaches 98.11\% at 87.28\% sparsity (1.20\% drop), LeViT-384 maintains 97.56\% at 54\% sparsity (0.44\% drop), and Swin-Tiny attains 87.64\% at 42\% sparsity (1.68\% drop). The results highlight that intelligently guiding existing techniques—not replacing them—offers a practical path to high-sparsity, hardware-efficient models suitable for edge deployment. Future work will extend the framework to larger datasets, structured pruning, and broader model families to further enhance its scalability and generality.
